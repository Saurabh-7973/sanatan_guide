What does dharmakṣetra mean?

Dharmakṣetra literally means "the field of dharma." It is a compound: dharma (right action, sacred duty) + kṣetra (field, place).

But the word does more than describe geography. Kurukṣetra was already a sacred plain in Vedic memory — a place where right and wrong were thought to be more visible, more decided. By calling it dharmakṣetra in the very first word of the Gītā, the text frames everything that follows as a moral question, not a military one.

‖१३·१‖ Bhagavad Gītā · Chapter 13
इदं शरीरं कौन्तेय क्षेत्रमित्यभिधीयते ।
"This body, O son of Kuntī, is called the kṣetra (field)..."

Krishna later expands the meaning of kṣetra in chapter 13 — the body itself becomes the field where dharma is practiced. So the opening "field of dharma" foreshadows a teaching that turns inward by the end of the text.

Why is Dhṛtarāṣṭra called blind?

Dhṛtarāṣṭra was born physically blind, but the Mahābhārata uses his blindness on multiple levels.

His blindness mirrors a moral blindness — an inability to see the harm his attachment to his sons has caused. By the time the Gītā opens, his refusal to restrain Duryodhana has led the kingdom to the edge of war.

Vyāsa offers him divine sight to witness the battle directly. He refuses. He chooses to hear Sañjaya's narration instead — distance preserved, accountability deferred. The blindness is, finally, a choice.

What changed and why

  • The verse is the personality of the screen, not the AI. A persistent saffron-anchored verse strip at the top — same leaf-thread vocabulary as Resume rows on Chapter List and Verse List — keeps the user oriented to which verse the conversation is about. Tap to return to the verse.
  • AI replies as flowing serif prose, not bubbles. User messages are rounded compact bubbles (right-aligned sans-serif). AI replies are typeset like commentary in Lora serif italic — the same register as commentary in Verse Detail. The asymmetry tells you "the AI is delivering thought; you're asking questions" without a single label.
  • No avatars, no AI sparkles, no "ChatGPT-style" gradient orbs. The AI is in service of the verse, not the show. Modern chat UI conventions, kept clean.
  • Verse-specific suggested questions, not generic chips. "Why is Dhṛtarāṣṭra called blind?" / "What does dharmakṣetra mean?" / "Who is Sañjaya?" — these are specific to BG 1.1. Each verse generates its own suggestion set. Teaches users what kinds of questions are worth asking.
  • Inline citation cards with the binding-line motif. When the AI references another verse from your offline corpus, it materializes as a small leaf — top binding line + diamond hole + verse coordinate (‖१३·१‖) + Sanskrit + translation. Tappable to navigate. This is your superpower: the AI cites verses from your actual library, not from training data.
  • Sanskrit terms inline highlighted in saffron Devanāgarī. When the AI uses a Sanskrit term in its English prose ("dharmakṣetra means..."), it renders in Tiro Devanagari with saffron color. Visible glossary on every line — readers can scan for the terms.
  • Reply footer with Save / Share / Regenerate. Standard chat actions, kept minimal. "Save" stores the AI explanation alongside the verse — turning the chat into a permanent commentary on the verse.
  • Sticky verse anchor with saffron leaf-thread. The 3px saffron strip on the left edge — same vocabulary as the Resume row on Chapter List and Verse List. The user always sees "you are anchored to this verse, you can return to it."
  • Input placeholder reflects scope: "Ask about this verse..." Not "Type a message..." or "Ask anything..." — the chat is bounded, and the placeholder communicates that bound.
  • Single typing indicator vocabulary across the app. Three saffron dots pulsing. Same on Verse Detail's "Explain this verse" loading state, on Search's Pandit-thinking state, here on AI Chat. System continuity for AI states throughout.
