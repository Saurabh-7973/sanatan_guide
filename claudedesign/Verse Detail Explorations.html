Verse detail — three design directions

The next room in the same temple as the home screen. Each direction inherits Temple Dawn's atmosphere, palette and type system; each adds its own atmospheric mechanic and a reading affordance the current screen doesn't have. Tokens only — saffron #E8820C, Tiro Devanagari, Lora, Outfit. Real data: Bhagavad Gītā 2.47. Light/dark toggle on every frame.

Five questions, answered before designing

  1. What is the user actually doing? Four jobs at four moments: reading (linear scroll, swipe to next verse), studying (looking up a word, jumping to commentary), memorising (silent re-reading of Sanskrit alone), contemplating (sitting with a single line, often the same line for days). The current screen treats all four as the same scroll. Design must make the contemplative mode and the study mode feel like different rooms, not a checklist.
  2. What does the current screen get wrong? A flat Translation on chip floating against the saffron horizon — it photographs as an ad. Sanskrit gets the same visual weight as the IAST that follows it; printed Gītās do the opposite. The numbered danda ॥११-३२॥ is hard-coded type, not a structural element. Padding around the Devanagari is luxurious but the letter-spacing isn't — the Sanskrit reads cramped. Bottom nav uses Material chevrons with no relationship to the temple language.
  3. What atmospheric layer is needed? Home has a horizon (sun rising over the page). The verse screen is the page. Three candidate atmospheres: a single oil-lamp glow (one Diwali lamp lighting the folio), a breathing svara (the saffron mark above the verse expands and contracts at 4.5s/breath), printed-press margin (the verse number bookmarked in deep red, like a published Gītā commentary).
  4. 2026 reading patterns to borrow. Readwise: tappable highlights that reveal definition inline (we adapt → padapāṭha). Kindle: chrome auto-fades on scroll (we adapt → meditation mode). Apple News+: pull-quote framing for one sentence. BibleHub: verse number as a typographic anchor, parallel translations stacked.
  5. Sanskrit/Hindu UX patterns earning their place. The ॥ double-danda as a visible, coloured glyph (not just a character). Padapāṭha (word-by-word) revealed inline instead of as a separate accordion — this is how a guru actually teaches a verse. Verse number rendered as a manuscript folio mark. Hindi as second translation, not third — most users are Hindi-first.

Lamp & Folio
