About the day

Vaiśākha Pūrṇimā marks three events in the life of the Buddha — his birth, his enlightenment, and his mahāparinirvāṇa. All three, tradition holds, fell on this same full moon.

For Hindus, Buddha is regarded as the ninth avatāra of Viṣṇu. The day is observed with fasting, charity, and the chanting of Buddha's teachings on suffering and liberation.

Practices
ॐ
Upavāsa
Fast from sunrise to moonrise. Some take only fruits and milk.
ॐ
Dāna
Charity to the poor — food, clothing, or alms.
ॐ
Pāṭha
Recitation of the Dhammapada or Buddha-stuti.

What changed and why

  • Almanac column layout, not festival cards. Each day is a horizontal row: Gregorian date + lunar phase circle + tithi/nakṣatra/festival. Mirrors the column structure of every Indian wall calendar (Kalnirnay, Bhaktivedanta, Mahalaxmi). The user reads the screen as a calendar, not as a list of cards.
  • Panchāṅga banner anchors the top. Today's tithi, nakṣatra, vāra, yoga, karaṇa — the five limbs (पञ्च-अङ्ग) of the Hindu calendar that give the screen its name. Most users in India use Gregorian dates daily but care about this for festivals; this surfaces it once and well.
  • Lunar phase circle as a real visual primitive. Each day shows a 16px circle with the moon's actual phase — Pūrṇimā is fully filled, Amāvasyā is empty, intermediate tithis show proportional fill. This is information visualization, not decoration: the user can scan a month and see the rhythm of the moon.
  • Iron-red ink for festival names. Real Hindu wall almanacs print sacred days in red ink (sindoor / kumkum color). I reserved --d-iron-red for this single purpose throughout the app — saffron is for state and accents; iron-red is only for festival markers. This means the user's eye instantly catches sacred days when scrolling.
  • Vikram Samvat year throughout. Month headers show "वैशाख · Vaiśākha · VS 2083" — locating the user in the actual Hindu calendar. Most apps strip this out; it's a key authenticity signal.
  • Filter strip surfaces tradition awareness. "All / Major parvas / Vrats / Regional" — recognizes that some festivals are pan-Hindu and some are tradition-specific. A user can filter to just major parvas (Diwali, Holi, etc.) or focus on vrats (Ekādaśī, Pradoṣa) or regional traditions (Onam, Pongal, Bihu).
  • Festival detail uses the structural-data block. Tithi, paksha, nakṣatra, tradition — the four data points that locate any festival in the calendar. Set in a 2x2 grid with both Devanāgarī and English. Underneath: prose about the day, practices (upavāsa/dāna/pāṭha), and verses to read.
  • Today gets a saffron leaf-thread on the left edge. Same vocabulary as the Resume rows from Chapter List and Verse List. System continuity throughout the app — the saffron thread always means "you are here."
  • No deity images, no carousel, no countdown timers. The brief was specific: "a wall calendar in a 1960s Indian home, almanac aesthetic, tithi/nakshatra/paksha as primitives." Modern card UI rejected on principle.
